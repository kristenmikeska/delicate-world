Tragedy in Incheon: U.S. National and CEO of Kim Company Found Dead in South Korean Warehouse

By Helena Marsh & Staff, International Reporting from the Ledger's Seoul Correspondent

Kim Sihyuk, 56, chief executive of Kim Company and one of the Upper East Side's most prominent business figures, was found dead Monday at a warehouse facility on the outskirts of Incheon, South Korea, in what Korean authorities are describing as an active criminal investigation. He had traveled to South Korea approximately two weeks prior.

Interpol confirmed Tuesday evening that it has opened a formal inquiry in coordination with the Korean National Police Agency. Multiple individuals associated with Kim Sihyuk's inner circle are currently being sought for questioning. A young man identified by Korean authorities as Kim Sihyuk's son — who holds dual U.S.-Korean citizenship and is understood to have accompanied his father on the trip to South Korea — has not been located. His whereabouts remain unknown. Interpol has issued a Red Notice in connection with his questioning.

The discovery was made early Monday morning by workers at an industrial facility near the port of Incheon. South Korean authorities confirmed that Kim Sihyuk was among multiple individuals found deceased at the scene. A preliminary statement from the Korean National Police Agency describes the incident as involving "a significant number of casualties" and notes that the investigation is international in scope. No official cause of death has been released.

Kim Company, which remains privately held and headquartered in Manhattan, had not issued a statement as of this edition's press time. Multiple phone lines at the company's offices went unanswered Tuesday morning. The company's registered communications office did not respond to messages left over a period of twelve hours.

The Upper East Side Castle community — which has been home to the Kim family for over two decades — received word of Sihyuk's death Tuesday through sources in the Korean business press. Representatives from Lee Industries, Moon Corporation, Bae Inc., and the Choi Group all declined to comment. The reaction among those who would speak was described by multiple sources as stunned silence.

Whether Kim Company can survive the death of its sole principal without a clear, named successor is a question for which there is no immediate answer. The company's founder leaves behind a $2.4 billion acquisition portfolio, active litigation in the Southern District of New York, and a son who is currently the subject of an international police inquiry. As of Tuesday night, no attorney has appeared publicly on behalf of the Kim family.

This is, by any measure, the most consequential development to touch the Castle in living memory. The Ledger will continue to report on this story as information becomes available.

Lee Industries vs. Kim Company: The Feud Reshaping New York's Corporate Landscape

By Helena Marsh, Senior Correspondent

In the autumn of 2005, Jaesang Lee and Kim Sihyuk stood together at the Grand Meridian and shook hands in front of every family that mattered on the Upper East Side. By the following November, the partnership was over. Twelve years later, the feud has never been more quietly ruthless.

The alliance they announced in 2005 was supposed to be the story of a generation — two of New York's most formidable corporate powers, aligned at last. It lasted fewer than twelve months. A VP was dead. The dissolution statement contained twenty-three words.

Twelve years have passed. The feud between Lee Industries and Kim Company has matured — from the dramatic and public to the quiet and surgical. Kim Company has made two attempts in the past decade to absorb Lee Industries-adjacent firms through undisclosed acquisition vehicles. Lee Industries has won three contracts that sources confirm Kim Company was actively pursuing. Each victory, each defeat, lands without comment from either principal.

Jaesang Lee has not spoken publicly about Kim Sihyuk in four years. Kim Sihyuk has never spoken publicly about Jaesang Lee.

"They don't talk about it because they don't need to," said one former Castle associate who has done business with both firms. "It's in every deal. It's in every room one of them is in and the other isn't."

The origins of the rupture have never been officially confirmed. Lee Industries declined to comment on the circumstances surrounding Dohyun Ji's 2006 death. Kim Company has never addressed it. The rest is Castle memory, passed between families at galas that both Lee and Kim attend — separately, on different evenings, as they have done for the past eleven years.

"They're playing chess," said one industry observer. "They've been playing it for thirteen years. The board is the whole New York business sector. Nobody's won yet."

The Silent Son: Why Jaehyun Lee Might Be the Most Formidable Lee Yet

By Helena Marsh, Senior Correspondent

He has never granted an interview. He has never appeared on a cover. He has no publicist, no verified public presence, and no official seat on Lee Industries' board — at least not yet. But in the rooms that matter, Jaehyun Lee has been present. And those who have sat across from him have begun to say the same thing: watch this one.

Jaehyun Lee is twenty-four years old. He is the eldest son of Jaesang Lee, chief executive of Lee Industries, and the second of five Lee siblings — though for the past five years, he has functioned as the eldest in practice, filling a space left by the 2013 departure of his older sister Solar. He graduated at the top of his class from one of the country's most competitive universities, declined multiple prestigious post-graduate opportunities that sources describe as "the kind of offers people don't typically decline," and returned to the Upper East Side with the purposefulness of someone who knew exactly what he had come back to do.

He has not been given a title. He has not been officially named to any executive role at Lee Industries. And yet he has been present — at the investor meetings, at the dinners that follow them, at the conversations that happen after those. "Jaehyun doesn't perform," said one source who has encountered him across multiple professional contexts. "That's the thing. Everyone around him is performing — his father performs, the other Castle sons perform. Jaehyun just operates."

Those who know him describe a quality of stillness. He reads a room quickly and says very little until he has something that needs saying. He is protective of Lee Industries and its public image in a way that colleagues call almost instinctive rather than strategic. He is, notably, notoriously private on the subject of his older sister's departure — a topic that any reporter attempting to profile him inevitably approaches, and that he has declined, in every instance, to discuss. "He watched his family absorb that," said one acquaintance. "And he decided very early that it would not happen again. Not on his watch."

Whether Jaehyun Lee will one day formally assume the role that most observers already believe is being prepared for him remains officially undetermined. But a company reflects, over time, the presence at its center — and Jaehyun Lee has been at the center of Lee Industries for longer than his title, or absence of one, would suggest. There is a version of this story where the succession is announced tomorrow. There is a version where it takes five years. In either case, those closest to the company say, Jaehyun will already be ready.
